# The Road to Carbon Neutral: Smith College Transportation Emissions.

## What is the purpose of this system?
Our visual analytics system was built to serve as a tool for the Center for the Environment, Ecological Design and Sustainability (CEEDS)  at Smith College. It was designed as an interactive article to be more accessible to its primary audience: faculty and students. Smith College, as part of the SIMAP greenhouse initiative, has collected data on campus emissions since 2004. Our VA system not only visualizes this data but does so in a way that tells the story of advancements and regressions in campus emissions at Smith College. It explains how the data was collected and for what purpose, while also describing overall emissions and transportation-related emissions. The system presents information on the amount of carbon emissions Smith produced each year since 2004, as well as breakdowns by variables such as emissions scope, faculty commuting, staff contributions, and directly sponsored air travel.
By the end of the VA experience, users come to understand that transportation, especially daily commuting, has a significant impact on emissions at Smith College, and that individual choices in this area can make a meaningful difference.
For context, our VA system includes written text intentionally structured to tell a story, along with visualizations in the “Overall Emissions” section. This section features a bar graph and a line graph showing carbon emissions at Smith College over time, broken down by scope. All data is measured in metric tons of CO₂, and this part of the system reveals how different sources contribute to the college’s overall footprint. Users can clearly observe the rise and fall of emissions over time.
Next, the system presents transportation emissions through a bar graph showing emissions at Smith College from 2009 to 2024, broken down by faculty, staff, and directly sponsored air travel. Accompanying this is a pie chart that appears when users click on the bar graph, illustrating the composition of emissions for a selected year.
Following these visualizations, the system includes text discussing the path forward for CEEDS, as well as an invitation for users to get involved in reducing emissions.
Overall, our VA system is designed to immerse users in the raw data collected by CEEDS in a way that is engaging, easy to digest, interactive, and story-driven. Its goal is not only to inform users about emissions at Smith College but also to help them recognize their role in reducing the campus’s carbon footprint.

## How can I use this system?
To use this system, users can interact with our two graph sections. The first bar chart, showing all three scopes of emissions, shows how much transportation emissions contribute to overall carbon emissions at Smith. To explore how these values change over the years, hover over the bars to get values and to highlight the corresponding area on the time series graph to its right. 

To learn more specifically about the scope 3 emissions coming from transportation, scroll down to the transportation section. There is a bar graph that presents transportation emissions from 2009 to 2024, broken down by faculty contributions, staff contributions, and directly sponsored air travel. Click on a bar of your choice to view a pie chart of the category breakdown for that specific year.

Lastly, if unsure how to interpret the data, read the text accompanying the graphs to understand what each represents and get a simple interpretation of the visualizations.  

## Known Bugs
This system works best on a larger browser window in order to see all the data correctly, and doesn’t display as well on smaller window sizes. 

## How was the Data Cleaned and Analyzed?
The data we received from CEEDS was quite organized and collected through Google Sheets. The biggest problem we ran into was sheets having inconsistent columns and rows that left spaces blank for ease of visual use; however came up as empty data spots in code. For this reason, some data cleaning was done manually through recreating and titling new CSVS. This manual editing was only possible due to the fact that this database was not too large and unmanageable.
 Our data mostly deals in “scopes” of emissions, or categories of how we classify the emissions created by Smith. In order to have the scopes separated for ease of visual encoding, we created three new CSVs from the original. This manual cleaning made our coding much more straightforward, so that we could access Scope 1, Scope 2, and Scope 3 through functions. The data included in Scope three emissions was more complicated than 1 and 2 and far more variables were included in categories. For instance, “Faculty Commuting” had several aspects to it (including Bike, Bus, Carpool, etc.). To create a clear separation between Scope 3, we created a folder that we could access its multiple files from. Scope 3 emissions were explored in greater detail through our transportation graphs, so this cleaning was very important to make a clear visual analysis of the specifics.
